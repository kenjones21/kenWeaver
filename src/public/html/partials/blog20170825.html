Transportation

More Public Transportation, Fewer Cars

Transportation constitutes Philadelphia's second biggest source of greenhouse gas emissions, after buildings. To this point, however, transportation has remained an intractable source of emissions, even in places like California where there has been substantial progress in reducing emissions.

A recent report from Drexel University details various scenarios by which the city can reduce its emissions. Somewhat disturbingly, only one of their scenarios for transportation reaches the 2050 target of an 80% reduction in emissions, and this scenario includes a large buildout of renewables, which would reach 49% of the electricity mix (from 2% currently) and nuclear, which would reach 40% of the energy mix (from 27% currently). Coupled with an increase in electricity demand that is necessary to decarbonize the energy system, this scenario represents a somewhat ambitious case for the electricity sector moving forward. However, it's not clear why the study chose 49% renewables as an optimistic case given that studies have shown 80% of renewable electricity is feasible for the US. In addition to this "optimistic" electricity mix, the scenario that reaches the 80% reduction in emissions also includes significant behavior change, with large increases in the public's use of walking, biking, and public transit. Given that this is probably policy over which the city has the most control, I'd like to focus on this part of policy.

As of right now (5:15 PM on a Tuesday) it would take me 20 minutes to drive to city hall. This is without considering the time to find a parking spot once I get there. Alternatively, I could bike (23 minutes) or take public transit (17 minutes). So then why do people drive in the city? One possible reason is the lack of safe infrastructure for bikes. For most travel paths in the city, the majority of your trip will not be protected. You may have a bike lane, but you will almost certainly not have any protection from cars who double park, or who are dropping someone off, or who simply drive erratically. Infrastructure is paid for by the city at large, despite there being only five vehicles per ten residents.
