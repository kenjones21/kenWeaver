Many of you will know that a recently made a rather abrupt career decision to leave graduate school. Part of this decision was a personal choice - I have struggled somewhat to focus on my research - but part of the decision was motivated by outside forces. Namely, over the past 8 months or so, I've been in a state of sort of sustained panic about climate change. I believe I can trace this feeling back to being introduced to a presentation by Kevin Anderson on carbon budgets, as absurd as that sounds. I've embedded the video below, but it's quite long so I'll try to walk through the logic in this post instead.

I suspect many people who have politics that are similar to mine probably have had climate change rattling around in their head for quite some time. It's difficult to really understand how serious it is - or how pressing - without understanding carbon budgets, and how they constrain the choices we make every day.

The last IPCC Report, finalized in 2014, reported a carbon budget for various temperature thresholds (1.5, 2, and 3 degrees Celsius). For these temperature targets, it details the amount of carbon dioxide the world can emit to have a certain chance at staying under that threshold. The international community has decided that 2 degrees Celsius is an appropriate threshold that delineates dangerous climate change from not so dangerous (acceptable?) climate change. There was a push by some countries to get a 1.5 degree target included during the latests negotiations in Paris, as for some places 2 degrees Celsius may well represent a death sentence, but for now we'll focus on the carbon budgets for two degrees.

For 2 degrees Celsius, the IPCC gives a budget of 1000 Gigatons of CO2 from 2011 on. Since we have a budget, we can work out how fast the world would need to decarbonize given delayed action to a certain date.
