Philadelphia's Climate Politics

The headline goal of the Paris agreement is as follows:

Holding the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2 째C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 째C above pre-industrial levels, recognizing that this would significantly reduce the risks and impacts of climate change;

Roughly speaking, the temperature that the earth reaches is contingent on cumulative emissions of carbon dioxide, with some leeway for other greenhouse gases given. This allows us to determine a budget we must live within if we want to stay below 2 degrees Celsius with a given probability. The above agreement says "well below 2 째C", but if we're charitable and read that "well below" as "a 66% chance of staying below" then the chart beneath demonstrates how fast our emissions must fall given action delayed to a certain date, assuming (again, charitably) an exponential rate of decarbonization.

The longer we wait, the faster we have to go, if we're serious about 2 degrees. This, then, is the lens through which our policies should be measured. Policies that merely hold our emissions constant, or those that give emissions reduction rates of 1 or 2 percent a year, are stunningly inadequate to the challenge at hand. When a politician (like our Mayor) symbolically signs on to the Paris agreement, then it ought to be contingent on them to demonstrate how they will get emissions to fall fast enough to be consistent with 2 째C. Symbolic gestures will mean little to the lives of young and vulnerable people around the planet.
