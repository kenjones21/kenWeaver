Philadelphia's Climate Politics

In the beginning of June, Donald Trump pulled the US out of the Paris agreement. This was rightly met with condemnations from leaders around the world, as well as many within the United States. In response, many city and state leaders stated that they would honor the Paris agreement, Jim Kenney among them. The headline goal of the Paris agreement is as follows:

Holding the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2 °C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels, recognizing that this would significantly reduce the risks and impacts of climate change;

Roughly speaking, the temperature that the earth reaches is contingent on cumulative emissions of carbon dioxide, with some leeway for other greenhouse gases given. This allows us to determine a budget we must live within if we want to stay below 2 °C with a given probability. The agreement says "well below 2 °C", but if we're charitable and read "well below" as "a 66% chance of staying below" then the chart beneath demonstrates how fast our emissions must fall given action delayed to a certain date, assuming (again, charitably) an exponential rate of decarbonization.

The longer we wait, the faster we have to go, if we're serious about 2 °C. This, then, is the lens through which our policies should be measured. Policies that merely hold our emissions constant, or those that give emissions reduction rates of 1 or 2 percent a year, are stunningly inadequate to the challenge at hand. When a politician (like our Mayor) says they will adhere to the Paris agreement, then it ought to be contingent on them to demonstrate how they will get emissions to fall fast enough to be consistent with 2 °C. Symbolic gestures will mean little to the lives of young and vulnerable people around the planet.

I'll have more about Philly's situation specifically in the next post, but the Mayor has made comments that the city's goal is 26-28% reduction by 2025, relative to 2005, echoing the US commitment to the Paris agreement. Needless to say, this has nothing to do with 2 °C.
