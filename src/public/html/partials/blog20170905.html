Transportation

Bike Lanes

Transportation constitutes Philadelphia's second biggest source of greenhouse gases, second to buildings. Transportation has remained an intractable source of emissions virtually everywhere in the US, even in places like California where there has been substantial progress in reducing emissions in general.

A recent report from Drexel University details various scenarios by which the city can reduce its emissions. Somewhat disturbingly, only one of their scenarios for transportation reaches the 2050 target of an 80% reduction in emissions, although their best case for the renewable share of the electricity mix is a somewhat mediocre 49% (see here and here for more optimistic cases), and if we can exceed that, the decarbonization challenge becomes somewhat easier. In addition to this "optimistic" electricity mix, and a large increase in the share of electric vehicles, the scenario that reaches the 80% reduction in emissions also includes significant behavior change, with large increases in the public's use of walking, biking, and public transit. Given that this is where local policy could prove most effective, I'd like to focus on it.

As of right now (5:15 PM on a Tuesday) it would take me 20 minutes to drive to city hall. This is without considering the time to find a parking spot once I get there. Alternatively, I could bike (23 minutes) or take public transit (17 minutes). So then why do so many people drive in the city? After all, some monthly car payments are comparable to lifetime costs for bikes, and public transit is similarly cheap in comparison. Of course, not all places in the city are equally accesible via public transit (although the bus system is quite extensive), so for those who prefer not to bike, a car may be the only option. But one can then beg the question "Why don't more people prefer to bike?"

One possible reason is the lack of safe infrastructure for bikes. Despite there being only five vehicles per ten residents, most infrastructure in the city is designed for automobile use, with little regard for the safety of cyclists. Designing a transportation system such that bicylists do not have to share a space with angry people wielding potentially lethal, multi-ton hunks of metal ought to be a bare minimum for encouraging a shift in transportation mode.

Visualization of bike lanes which are protected by a physical barrier; more info here. Roads in light blue have either unprotected bike lanes or no bike lanes at all.

To give the city some credit, there has been (mostly rhetorical) progress, but it has been agonizingly slow to this point, and the city only plans to pursue funding to make the protected bike lanes more permanent in 2019. Given that the task force was established with the goal of eliminating traffic fatalities by 2030, three years to begin to pursue funding seems rather inadequate.

While the city is pursuing Vision Zero to improve public health by preventing traffic injuries and fatalities, it's important to realize this is also an issue of equity. Designing a transportation system around a machine that costs tens of thousands of dollars necessarily makes life more difficult for the city's poorest residents. Speaking more broadly, Philadelphia's situation with bike lanes is indicative of other issues where decarbonization can be coupled to measures that make society more equitable, bring health benefits to communities, and generally make our city more livable for the people that reside here, with the added benefit of slowing down climate change and giving future generations hope that they might live on a healthy planet.
